    Instructions

    Moneyopoly: the history of money

    Money is the good or tool people use to live. It helps families trade their work for the essentials: shelter, food, and water.

    1. Barter: trade goods directly. It only works when both people want exactly what the other has.
    2. Commodity Money: shells become a common good that many traders accept.
    3. Gold / Hard Commodity Money: scarce, durable money makes saving easier.
    4. Fiat / Paper Money: governments can issue paper money, but it can be seized, controlled, or printed.
    5. Bitcoin / Digital Electronic Money: events still happen, but the money itself cannot be printed away or destroyed by a local ruler.

    Every 3 rounds there is a crisis or event, like in real life. The question is: can this form of money survive environmental events such as earthquakes and fires, political events such as dictatorships or leader seizure, economic downturns, and money printing?

    The scorecard checks the same questions every time: survival, failed trades, purchasing power, debasement, seizure/freeze risk, and how easy the money was to carry, divide, and use.

    The Bitcoin board is designed to show the hardest-money idea: earthquakes, fires, politics, and downturns can still hurt people and markets, but Bitcoin itself is not debased by money printing.
    Final comparison

    Same needs, same crises, different money

    Each board asks Maya to secure food, water, and shelter. The difference is the money system: barter matching, commodity acceptance, gold hardness, fiat trust, or Bitcoin self-custody.
    Teacher / facilitator mode

    Run-of-show

    1. Start with Barter: ask why good goods are not always good money.
    2. Move to Commodity Money: ask what changed when sellers accepted shells.
    3. Play Gold: ask why scarcity/durability helped saving, then discuss weight and confiscation.
    4. Play Fiat: ask how stimulus can feel good first and then raise prices.
    5. Play Bitcoin: ask what problems fixed supply solves and what responsibilities self-custody adds.

    Discussion prompts

    • Which board made it easiest to get essentials?
    • Which money held purchasing power through crises?
    • Who could change, seize, freeze, or debase the money?
    • Was the money easy to carry, divide, verify, and use?
