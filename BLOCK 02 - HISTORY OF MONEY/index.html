Block 2

History of Money

Money was not invented in a boardroom. It emerged because people needed better ways to trade, save, measure value, and coordinate across larger communities.

Start lessonActivities
Flagship experience

The market teaches the lesson.

Start with people, goods, and wants. Add complexity until the need for money becomes obvious, then compare commodity money, receipts, fiat, digital money, and Bitcoin.

Web lesson

Walk through the market plaza and feel why better money keeps emerging.

1

Barter gets stuck

Joseph has bananas and wants coconut. Yael has coconut but wants mango. Tammy has mango but wants papaya. Nobody gets what they want because the wants do not line up.

2

A saleable good appears

Groups eventually notice that a commonly accepted item can be traded even if they do not personally want it. That item becomes a bridge between trades.

3

Commodity money improves trade

Shells, salt, cattle, gold, and silver all solved some problems and created others. Learners compare portability, divisibility, durability, scarcity, and recognizability.

4

Receipts and fiat add trust

Paper receipts made heavy metal easier to move, but the system depended on the issuer holding the backing. When backing weakens or disappears, trust shifts to institutions and laws.

5

Bitcoin enters the comparison

Bitcoin is introduced after the problem is felt: a scarce digital money that can move online without a central issuer. Learners compare it to the earlier forms instead of memorizing a slogan.

6

Conference mode

Assign audience volunteers as market characters, run one failed barter round, introduce a saleable good, then ask the room what changed. This scales from classroom to large event.

Facilitator flow

Apply

Good-money comparison

Ask each group to defend which money works best for an island, a city, international trade, and internet payments.
