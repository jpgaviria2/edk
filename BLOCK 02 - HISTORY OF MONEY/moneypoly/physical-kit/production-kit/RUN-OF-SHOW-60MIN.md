# 60-Minute Run of Show

## Room format
- 8 tables
- 5 teams of 2 per table
- one table banker covers two tables

## How to think about the room
- each table is a **self-contained mini-market**
- each team at the table represents a household, producer, or trader
- teams mostly trade **within their own table**
- the room stays synchronized by era, not by one giant shared board
- optional: later eras can include short cross-table exchange windows

## Objective to say out loud

"Your job is not just to survive today. Your job is to secure food, water, and shelter, preserve savings through crises, and leave something durable for the next generation." 

## Minute-by-minute

### 0:00-0:05 — Welcome + rules
Lead facilitator:
- explain survival first, generational wealth second
- explain that each table represents a small economy
- explain that crises will change conditions
- tell teams not to overthink before play

AV:
- project Moneypoly home / teacher mode
- show countdown timer

### 0:05-0:13 — Phase 1: Barter
Table teams trade directly.

Facilitator prompts:
- was trade easy?
- did having value guarantee a trade?
- what broke down?

Bankers:
- enforce direct barter only
- log failed trades roughly

### 0:13-0:19 — Phase 2: Changed needs
Introduce next-generation needs and a scarcity shock.

Actions:
- every team gets a new need
- event master triggers one scarcity card per banker's pair of tables

Prompt:
- did future planning change your behaviour?

### 0:19-0:27 — Phase 3: Commodity money
Introduce shells first. Optionally show coffee beans and beads as competing monies.

Actions:
- tables begin accepting shells
- bankers explain that not all tables must accept every commodity equally at first

Prompt:
- what changed when sellers accepted a common medium?

### 0:27-0:34 — Phase 4: Gold
Introduce gold coins and larger savings logic.

Actions:
- bankers swap shell-heavy savings into gold at posted rates
- event master triggers bandit or clipping at selected tables

Prompt:
- why is gold better for savings than mangoes?
- why is it worse for everyday use?

### 0:34-0:42 — Phase 5: Credit & Ledgers
Introduce bank branches, signed slips, and 10% fee.

Actions:
- one banker acts as Medici branch
- teams may deposit gold and receive signed slips
- ledger entries become mandatory

Prompt:
- why do people accept paper claims?
- what happens if trust breaks?

### 0:42-0:49 — Phase 6: Fiat
Introduce notes, price shocks, and freeze risk.

Actions:
- facilitator injects new fiat to some tables first
- prices rise visibly on mini price boards
- one freeze/confiscation event fires

Prompt:
- did more notes make people richer or just change prices?

### 0:49-0:56 — Phase 7: Bitcoin
Introduce sats, self-custody, hot wallet vs cold storage.

Actions:
- teams choose hot wallet or cold storage
- node verification card cancels one fake/faulty claim
- money printer event has no effect on sats supply

Prompt:
- what crises still hurt people?
- what crises fail to debase the money?

### 0:56-1:00 — Final debrief
Score teams and summarize:
- survival
- savings durability
- seizure/freeze resistance
- portability/divisibility
- whether value could reach the next generation

Close with:
"Money is not just what helps you buy today. The deepest question is what preserves the fruits of your work across time." 

## Banker logging rule
You do not need perfect accounting. You need enough signal to compare:
- failed trades in barter
- ease of exchange with commodity money
- loss risk in gold
- trust dependence in ledgers
- debasement/freeze risk in fiat
- self-custody tradeoffs in Bitcoin
